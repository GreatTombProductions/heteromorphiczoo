## Stolen Decision 1: The Industry Extraction Model (What HZ Replaces)

The standard music merch pipeline is an extraction chain. Every link takes a cut:

### Production Economics (Industry Standard)

| Item | Production Cost | Typical Retail | Artist Margin (D2C) | Artist Margin (via Company) |
|------|----------------|---------------|---------------------|---------------------------|
| T-shirt | $8–20 | $25–35 | 50–70% | 25–35% of wholesale |
| Hoodie/Sweater | $20–40 | $50–65 | 40–55% | 20–30% |
| Patch/Pin/Sticker | $1–3 | $5–12 | 70–85% | Varies |
| Poster (silk-screened) | $8–15 | $20–30 | 50–65% | 25–35% |
| Hat | $12–20 | $25–35 | 45–60% | 20–30% |
| Vinyl (special pressing) | $10–15 | $25–40 | 50–65% | Lower via label |

### Revenue Split Models (Industry Standard)

**Merch Company Deal (Traditional):**
- Artist receives **75–80% of net profit** after the company deducts manufacturing, freight, CC fees, sales tax, and hall fees
- Merchandiser keeps **20–25%** for handling production/logistics
- Advances are **recoupable** — artist owes them back regardless of sales
- Some companies charge interest on advances
- Tour must begin within ~3 months of signing

**Label-Mediated Merch (MerchNow pattern):**
- Label keeps **50%** of merch revenue
- MerchNow keeps **20%** as platform/fulfillment fee
- Artist receives **30%** — the worst end of the extraction chain
- Artist provides designs; label and platform extract the value

**Venue Cuts (Live Merch):**
- Venues take **10–35%** of gross merch sales (typically 25–30%)
- "Merch cuts" are the most contentious issue in live music economics
- Hard merch (CDs/vinyl): lower commission
- Soft merch (shirts/hats): higher commission
- Additional costs: merch sellers ($75–200/show or $20–25/hr), manager commission (5–20% of net)

**Print-on-Demand (Nightshift Merch / IndieMerchstore model):**
- Company handles fulfillment, warehousing, web store
- Artist/label provides designs
- Company sets wholesale minimums ($200+ at Nightshift)
- Wholesale only to physical stores or established resale sites
- No transparency into per-unit economics from artist's perspective

**HelloMerch (indie-friendly variant):**
- Flat rates, no contracts, artists retain all rights
- "Started in 2008 as an outlet for bands to manufacture and sell merchandise without giving up their rights"
- Still a fulfillment service — artist pays production + fulfillment fees, company handles logistics
- Better terms than label-mediated, but still a service-for-fee extraction model

### What the Extraction Model Optimizes For

The entire industry optimizes for **margin per unit**. Every actor in the chain — manufacturer, fulfillment company, label, venue, manager — is extracting a percentage. The artist's question is always "how much of this $30 t-shirt do I actually keep?" The answer, depending on channel: **$4 to $15**.

The structural assumption: merch is a revenue line. The artist needs merch income to survive. Therefore, margin extraction is tolerable because the alternative is no merch at all.

## Stolen Decision 2: The Etsy Maker Ecosystem (What HZ Adapts)

Etsy's "Production Partners" system is the closest existing framework to what HZ wants, but designed for a different relationship.

### Etsy Production Partner Rules

- **Disclosure required:** If someone else physically produces your design, they must be listed as a production partner
- **Applies to:** Print-on-demand companies, contracted manufacturers, anyone who isn't a direct employee
- **The relationship:** Brand designs → maker produces → brand sells under brand's shop
- **Who owns the customer:** The brand (shop owner), not the maker

### Brand-Maker Collaboration Models (from Etsy ecosystem)

**Standard Etsy collaboration:**
- Brand provides design/specification
- Maker produces to spec
- Brand handles listing, marketing, customer service
- Maker receives production fee (cost + margin)
- No ongoing revenue share — it's a wholesale purchase

**Creator-Brand Partnership (emerging 2025-2026):**
- Flat fee for agreed-upon deliverables
- Retainers ($1,000–$10,000/month for ongoing production)
- Revenue-sharing structures emerging but rare
- Equity partnerships with vesting schedules (very rare, mostly in influencer space)

### What Etsy Gets Right for HZ

1. **Maker attribution exists.** The production partner framework acknowledges that someone else made the thing. HZ takes this further — the maker is the *feature*, not a disclosure requirement.
2. **Handmade/custom positioning.** Etsy's market identity is "unique, custom, handmade." HZ's merch wants exactly this energy.
3. **Direct maker relationships.** Etsy sellers are often individuals or small studios. This is the partner pool.

### What Etsy Gets Wrong for HZ

1. **The brand→maker direction is inverted.** Etsy assumes the brand provides the design and the maker executes. HZ wants makers who bring their *own* craft and aesthetic, interpreted through HZ's visual language.
2. **No ongoing revenue share by default.** Etsy's production partner model is wholesale purchase. HZ wants ongoing partnership with shared upside.
3. **The brand owns the customer.** In the Etsy model, the shop owner captures the customer relationship. HZ wants *cross-pollination* — the maker's audience finds HZ, HZ's audience finds the maker.

## Stolen Decision 3: Brand Licensing Agreement Structure (What HZ Steals and Inverts)

Standard brand licensing is the legal framework for "I own IP, you make stuff with it, we split the money." This is the skeleton HZ needs — but the economics are inverted.

### Standard Licensing Terms (Industry)

**Royalty Structures:**
- 5–10% of net revenue is standard for brand/trademark licensing
- Tiered rates common: 5% up to $2M, 7.5% $2M–$5M, 10% above $5M
- Minimum guarantees: licensee agrees to a floor payment regardless of sales
- Advances: upfront lump sums, typically recoupable against future royalties

**Agreement Types:**
- **Non-exclusive** (most common): Licensor can grant rights to multiple partners
- **Exclusive** (premium): Sole rights within territory or product category — commands higher royalties
- A year is a common starting duration

**Key Clauses:**
- Quality control: Licensor retains approval rights over all products
- IP protection: All goodwill from trademarks remains with licensor
- Territory restrictions: Geographic limits on where licensee can sell
- Product specifications: Detailed requirements for what the licensed product looks like
- Termination conditions: What triggers the end of the arrangement

**The Standard Direction:**
Brand (licensor) → Maker (licensee). Brand owns IP, maker pays to use it. Revenue flows: consumer → maker → royalty to brand.

### What HZ Steals

The *structure* of a licensing agreement is useful: quality control, IP protection, territory, termination. These clauses exist because they solve real problems (what if the maker produces garbage with your name on it?). Steal the clause structure.

### What HZ Inverts

**Standard model:** Maker pays brand for permission to use IP. Brand extracts royalty.

**HZ model:** Brand pays maker upfront compensation *plus* ongoing revenue share. Revenue flows: consumer → HZ → share to maker. The maker never pays HZ. HZ pays the maker.

This is the inversion that makes the pitch unusual. In standard licensing, the IP owner monetizes their brand by taxing others' production. HZ monetizes nothing — it *subsidizes* production because the goal isn't revenue extraction, it's pattern propagation.

## Stolen Decision 4: The Contrast Frame (HZ's Positioning)

The council identified the right strategy: "the contrast frame is more powerful than the standalone assertion." Here's the concrete contrast:

### The Comparison Table

| Dimension | Industry Standard | HZ Partner Program |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Who pays whom** | Maker pays brand (licensing fee/royalty) | Brand pays maker (upfront + rev share) |
| **Revenue direction** | Consumer → Maker → Royalty to brand | Consumer → HZ → Share to maker |
| **Design ownership** | Brand provides design, maker executes | Maker brings their craft, HZ provides creative direction |
| **Maker visibility** | Disclosed as "production partner" (footnote) | Featured as the *reason to buy* (hero) |
| **Goal** | Maximize margin per unit | Maximize cultural propagation per unit |
| **Loss tolerance** | Zero — every item must be profitable | Negative margins acceptable ("every shirt costs me $5 net") |
| **Customer relationship** | Brand owns customer, maker is invisible | Cross-pollination — both audiences see each other |
| **Scale model** | Volume production, lower unit cost | Small batch, higher craft value, limited runs |
| **Partner selection** | Who can produce cheaply at scale? | Whose aesthetic extends our cultural territory? |
| **Contract structure** | Licensing agreement (maker pays royalty) | Partnership agreement (HZ pays upfront + share) |
| **Exclusivity** | Often required (maker can't work with competitors) | Not required (maker's other work is their business) |
| **Quality control** | Brand approves everything (top-down) | Collaborative — HZ provides direction, maker brings expertise |

### The Pitch Sentence

> "Here's how brand-maker partnerships usually work: the brand licenses their name, the maker pays for the privilege, and the brand extracts a royalty from every sale. Here's how ours works: we pay you upfront, we share revenue from every sale, and we feature your craft as the reason our fans should buy. We're not building a merch line. We're building a network of artisans whose work our audience should know about."

## Stolen Decision 5: Compensation Structure for HZ Partners

Synthesized from licensing frameworks, creator partnership models, and the council's economic constraints.

### Proposed Structure

**Upfront Payment:**
- Fixed fee per collection/batch, paid before production begins
- Covers the maker's materials + time + a premium
- Range: depends on item complexity — suggested $200–$1,000 per design/batch based on production costs seen in the landscape
- Key: the maker never risks their own capital on this partnership

**Revenue Share:**
- Percentage of retail sale price (not net profit — simpler, more transparent)
- Suggested: 15–25% of retail to the maker
- HZ retains the rest for production costs, shipping, platform fees, and the net-negative margin Ray specified
- Revenue share is ongoing for as long as the item sells

**No Minimums, No Exclusivity:**
- Maker isn't locked in — they can work with anyone
- HZ isn't committed to minimum order quantities
- Either party can end the arrangement with reasonable notice
- This is the structural signal that the relationship is collaborative, not extractive

**IP Treatment:**
- Maker retains full rights to their original designs and craft
- HZ retains rights to HZ-specific branding elements (logo, name, visual language)
- Collaborative pieces: jointly owned, either party can display in portfolio
- All goodwill from HZ trademarks stays with HZ (standard licensing clause — steal this)

### Why This Works at Negative Margins

Ray's constraint: "every shirt costs me $5 net." The math:

- T-shirt production: ~$15 (premium quality, small batch)
- Upfront maker payment (amortized per unit): ~$5
- Revenue share to maker (20% of $30 retail): ~$6
- Shipping/platform fees: ~$5
- Total cost per unit: ~$31
- Retail price: $30
- **Net: -$1 per unit** (before accounting for upfront design fee)

This is sustainable because HZ's economics are disentangled from band revenue. The negative margin is the *strategy* — it funds pattern propagation with income from consulting, not from merch sales. The moment a band needs merch to be profitable, this model is impossible. That's the moat.

## Stolen Decision 6: The Outreach Framework

Demiurge needs outreach materials. The council specified a "Why You" template that makes each outreach a cultural argument, not a form letter. Here's the framework stolen from creator partnership best practices, inverted for HZ's model:

### Outreach Template Structure

**Element 1: The Hook (Why We Found You)**
Specific to this maker. Not "we love your work" — *what specifically* about their craft resonates with HZ's aesthetic. "Your ironwork has the same relationship to mass-produced jewelry that our production approach has to sample-replaced music."

**Element 2: The Inversion (How This Is Different)**
One paragraph explaining the HZ model. The contrast frame. "Most band merch programs extract margin from makers. Ours doesn't. We pay you upfront, share revenue, and feature your craft as the reason to buy."

**Element 3: The Offer (What We're Proposing)**
Specific collaboration concept. Not "make us some stuff" — a concrete idea. "We'd love to commission a limited run of [specific item] that interprets [specific HZ visual element] in your medium."

**Element 4: The Terms (Quick & Clean)**
- Upfront payment for the batch
- X% revenue share on every sale
- Full attribution on our /relics page and all marketing
- No exclusivity — your other work is your business
- You retain rights to your designs

**Element 5: The Why (Why This Matters)**
The cultural argument. Not business case — *values alignment*. "We believe the people making things with their hands deserve the same visibility as the musicians. Our fans understand craft. They'll understand yours."

### Partner Selection Criteria (for Demiurge's prospect list)

| Criterion | What to Look For |
|-----------|-----------------|
| **Craft alignment** | Does their medium extend HZ's aesthetic territory? (metalwork, leather, dark/gothic aesthetics, occult imagery, etc.) |
| **Scale fit** | Small batch, handmade, not mass-production. The partnership should feel artisanal. |
| **Existing audience** | Do they have their own following? Cross-pollination requires two audiences. |
| **Platform presence** | Active Etsy/Instagram/website. Evidence of ongoing craft, not a dormant shop. |

## External Assumptions That Break

Applying the standard heist evaluation — what external assumptions don't transfer:

1. **Revenue-positive assumption.** Every merch framework assumes the goal is profit. HZ's goal is propagation at negative margins. All compensation calculations need to be evaluated against "does Ray net-lose ~$5/item" not "is this profitable."

2. **Brand-as-licensor assumption.** Standard licensing puts the IP owner in the extraction seat. HZ needs the legal *structure* of licensing (quality control, IP protection) without the *economics* of licensing (royalty extraction from maker).

3. **Scale assumption.** Industry merch economics are optimized for volume (lower unit cost at scale). HZ's model is optimized for craft value (higher unit cost, lower volume, cultural signal per unit).

4. **Platform dependency assumption.** Industry models route through Shopify, Etsy, MerchNow. HZ routes through its own `/relics` page — owned surface, owned customer relationship, maker attribution as feature.
