## Section 1: Program Structure

### The Thesis

Heteromorphic Zoo's merch program is not a revenue line. It is a propagation network.

Every band merch program in the industry operates on the same assumption: merch exists to generate revenue. The entire supply chain — manufacturer, fulfillment company, label, venue — is organized around extracting margin from each unit sold. The artist's question is always "how much of this $30 t-shirt do I actually keep?"

HZ's question is different: "whose craft should our audience encounter next?"

The program exists to connect the menagerie with artisans whose work extends the Zoo's cultural territory. The economics are inverted: HZ pays makers upfront, shares revenue from sales, and accepts negative margins as the designed outcome. This is sustainable because HZ's economics are disentangled from band revenue — the negative margin is funded by consulting income, not by music sales. The moment a band needs merch to be profitable, this model is impossible. That disentanglement is the structural moat.

### The Triangle

The partner program is the third surface of a three-surface posture:

| Surface | What it proves | Where it lives |
|---------|---------------|----------------|
| **Chronicle** | The music is 100% human | /chronicle |
| **AI Policy** | The stance is active, not performative | /policy |
| **Relics** | The economics back the words | /relics |

The AI policy says "every note is human." The Chronicle provides the receipt. The Relics page proves the same principle extends beyond music into every physical artifact bearing the Zoo's name. When the /relics page says "every piece is made by someone whose craft we believe in," the policy and the Chronicle have already established that this band means what it says.

### What "Relics" Means

Not "merch." Not "shop." Not "store." Relics — objects with provenance, artifacts of craft, things that carry the mark of who made them. The word itself is the cultural argument. A relic is not consumed; it is kept. The name signals that these objects have more in common with the offerings in the gallery than with a print-on-demand t-shirt.

## Section 2: Compensation Terms

*Revised 2026-04-28 per Council ace0e247 — Ray's updated economic model. Previous version assumed HZ-side fulfillment + 20% revenue share. New model is radically simpler.*

### The Model

**One component: upfront payment for design work, flowing from HZ to the maker.**

| Item Complexity | Suggested Range | What It Covers |
|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| Simple (patches, pins, stickers) | $150–$300 | Design work + initial prototype |
| Moderate (apparel, prints, leather goods) | $300–$700 | Design work + craft premium |
| Complex (metalwork, ceramics, multi-piece) | $500–$1,000 | Design work + craft premium + tooling |

The upfront payment covers the maker's design work — the creative collaboration that produces an HZ-themed piece in their medium. After that, the maker owns the entire production and fulfillment chain.

**No revenue share. No ongoing per-sale payments.** This bounds HZ's exposure completely — a piece that goes viral costs HZ nothing additional. The upfront payment is the total financial commitment per partnership.

### Post-Design: Partner Handles Everything

Once the design collaboration is complete:
- **Production** is the partner's decision (batch size, materials, timing)
- **Pricing** is the partner's decision (they know their margins)
- **Stock levels** are the partner's decision (they know their capacity)
- **Shipping and fulfillment** are the partner's responsibility
- **Payment infrastructure** is the partner's (their Etsy, Shopify, Square, whatever they already use)

HZ's website is a **thin wrapper** on however they do business. Each partner card on `/relics` links to the partner's own storefront. We are a showcase, not a storefront.

**Why this works:** The partner already knows how to sell their products. They have their own shop, their own shipping workflow, their own pricing model. Adding HZ's logistics on top of that creates friction for both sides. The thin-wrapper model means: we pay for the design, we showcase the result, we link to where people can buy it. Zero ongoing operational burden on either side.

**Volume transparency:** As a niche extreme metal band, order volume may be low. Partners should be prepared to produce the design, then hold it in their back pockets for months before an order comes in. The partnership is long-term positioning, not immediate revenue.

**Trust model:** Tit-for-tat. We trust partners will fulfill orders when they come. Partners who fail to ship lose their showcase spot. No contracts, no lawyers, no SLAs.

### Structural Terms

| Term | Detail |
|------|--------|
| **Exclusivity** | None. The maker's other work is their business. |
| **Minimums** | None. No minimum order quantities from either side. |
| **Duration** | Open-ended. Either party can end with 30 days' notice. |
| **IP — maker's work** | Maker retains full rights to their original designs and techniques. |
| **IP — HZ branding** | HZ retains rights to name, logo, and visual language. |
| **IP — collaborative pieces** | Jointly owned. Either party can display in portfolio. |
| **Quality approval** | Collaborative. HZ provides creative direction (see Brand Guidelines, Section 3). Maker brings expertise. Not top-down approval — a conversation. |
| **Attribution** | Full. Maker's name, process description, and portfolio link on /relics page and all marketing. |
| **Payment timing** | Upfront payment before design work begins. No ongoing payments. |

### What This Is Not

- Not a licensing agreement (the maker never pays HZ)
- Not a wholesale purchase (we don't buy inventory)
- Not a fulfillment partnership (we don't handle shipping, stock, or payment processing)
- Not a commission with ongoing obligations (upfront payment is the total commitment)
- Not employment (no exclusivity, no control over their other work)

The closest framework is a **sponsored showcase** — HZ pays for the creative collaboration and provides marketing reach. The partner handles everything commercial. The economic model is sustainable because HZ's exposure is bounded (upfront payment only) and the propagation value (audience cross-pollination) is the return on investment.

## Section 3: Brand Guidelines for Partners

### What Partners Must Follow

**1. Color palette compliance.** Any HZ-branded element (logo, name, tagline) must use the established palette. Partners receive a brand asset kit with hex values, approved logo files, and usage examples.

| Element | Required Color | Notes |
|---------|---------------|-------|
| HZ logo on merch | `--hz-bone` (#e8ddd0) on dark, `--hz-plum` (#1a0e1e) on light | No color modifications to the logo |
| HZ name in text | `--hz-bone` or `--hz-gold` (#c49a3c) | Gold for emphasis on special pieces |
| Background/surface | Any palette color | Must feel warm, not neutral grey |

**2. Logo usage.** The HZ logo appears on collaborative pieces but does not dominate. The maker's mark or signature should have equal or greater visual presence. The partnership is "your craft, our name alongside it" — not "our logo on your product."

**3. No modifications to the logo.** No stretching, recoloring, adding effects, or placing on backgrounds that reduce legibility. The logo is artwork, not a stamp.

### What Partners Are Free To Do

**Everything else.** The creative direction is collaborative, not prescriptive. HZ provides:

- The visual language as reference (palette, typography samples, site screenshots, artwork)
- The aesthetic territory description (stained-glass luminosity, ecclesiastical gravity, painterly warmth)
- Specific direction for each collaboration ("we'd love a pendant that interprets the portal motif")

The maker interprets this through their medium and expertise. A blacksmith doesn't need to replicate the Lordigan artwork in iron — they bring their understanding of metalwork to the territory HZ occupies. The result should feel like it belongs in the same cathedral, not like it was manufactured to spec.

### The Aesthetic Territory (For Partner Reference)

Language for the brand asset kit that communicates the visual world without requiring partners to read the full design spec:

> **The world of Heteromorphic Zoo:**
>
> Imagine a cathedral made of stained glass — not dark and austere, but luminous. Light pouring through deep magentas, violets, crimsons, and golds. The palette is warm, painterly, tinted. There are no neutral greys. Every shadow is colored. Every surface has texture — grain, brushwork, the feeling of something made by hand.
>
> The register is liturgical: reverent but never self-serious. Sacred text inscribed into walls built to last, with the dry confidence of something holy that knows it's also absurd. Worship music for monsters.
>
> Your work doesn't need to replicate this world. It needs to feel at home in it. A hand-forged pendant, a hand-dyed garment, a hand-thrown chalice — these already share the essential quality: they were made by someone who cares about the relationship between maker and material. That's the connection.

## Section 4: Partner Selection Criteria

### Five Criteria, Priority Order

**1. Aesthetic Resonance**

Does the maker's existing work — before any HZ collaboration — share something with the Zoo's visual and conceptual territory? Not "can they produce our logo on a product" but "does their natural aesthetic live in the same neighborhood?"

*The diagnostic:* Look at their portfolio with the visual design spec in mind. Could their work exist in the same cathedral? If their style would require them to become someone they're not to fit HZ's world, there isn't resonance. If their work already feels adjacent — dark, textured, warm, handmade, intentional — there is.

**2. Craft Authenticity**

Is the maker's process itself a statement about craft? The process matters because the process IS the cultural message. The AI policy says "every note is human." The relics should say "every stitch is deliberate." The maker's process should be as documentable and specific as the band's production process in the Chronicle.

*The diagnostic:* Can you describe their process in a paragraph that reads like a Chronicle entry? "Forged at [temperature] using [technique], each piece takes [time] and shows [distinctive quality]." If the process description sounds generic ("printed on high-quality material"), the craft signal is too weak for this program.

**3. Cross-Pollination Potential**

Does the maker have their own audience? The partner program is a trade route — HZ's menagerie encounters the maker's work, the maker's audience encounters HZ. A maker with no existing audience is a commission (fine as a one-off). A maker with their own following is a cross-pollination node.

*The diagnostic:* Do they have an active shop (Etsy/standalone), an Instagram with engaged followers, or a community presence? "Active" means recent work, recent posts, evidence of ongoing craft — not a dormant shop from 2019.

**4. Values Alignment**

Does the maker's existing self-presentation signal shared values about authenticity, human-made work, and transparency? They don't need to have published an AI policy. Look at implicit signals: the shop description that says "every piece is handmade in my studio," the Instagram bio that names their materials and techniques, the about page that describes their journey.

*The diagnostic:* Would this maker's own values statement, if they wrote one, sound compatible with "every note is human"? If their operation involves drop-shipping, mass production outsourced to anonymous factories, or misrepresenting handmade status — they're not the partner.

**5. Medium Diversity**

The partner portfolio should represent breadth. If all initial partners are blacksmiths, the portfolio signals "HZ has a metal fetish" not "HZ believes in craft across mediums." The medium diversity is itself a cultural statement: craft transcends material.

*The diagnostic:* After selecting each partner, ask: does the current portfolio cover multiple mediums? Target at least 3 distinct mediums across the initial 3-5 partners.

## Section 5: Initial Prospect Categories

The first 3-5 partnerships should demonstrate the program's range and establish the /relics page with enough variety to feel curated, not sparse. Each category includes the cultural argument for that medium's connection to HZ.

### Category 1: Metalsmith / Blacksmith

**What:** Pendants, rings, belt buckles, ritual objects in occult/gothic aesthetic. Hand-forged, not cast from molds.

**Why this medium:** The relationship between a blacksmith and their forge is the oldest version of the relationship between a musician and their instrument. Heat, pressure, physical engagement with resistant material. The metaphor writes itself — and it's not a metaphor. It's the same thing.

**What to look for:** Active Etsy shop or personal site. Process documentation (forge photos, WIP shots). Existing work in dark/gothic/occult territory. Small batch sizes (not bulk jewelry manufacturing).

**Example pitch angle:** "Your ironwork has the same relationship to mass-produced jewelry that our production approach has to sample-replaced music."

### Category 2: Leather Worker

**What:** Custom patches, journal covers, guitar straps, wallets, accessories. Hand-tooled, hand-dyed, hand-stitched.

**Why this medium:** Leather work is one of the few crafts where the material's history is literally visible — the grain, the patina, the way it ages. A leather piece from 2026 will look different in 2030. It records time. The Chronicle documents when things were made; leather shows it.

**What to look for:** Tooling and dyeing skill visible in portfolio. Custom work (not just stamped patterns). Gothic, medieval, or dark aesthetic work. Evidence of commission experience (comfortable working to a creative brief).

**Example pitch angle:** "The way your leather ages — every scratch, every patina mark — is the physical version of what our Chronicle does for our music. A record of time that the material itself holds."

### Category 3: Textile Artist

**What:** Hand-dyed or hand-printed limited-run apparel. NOT screen printing on blanks — that's the commodity version. Shibori, block printing, natural dyeing, hand-embroidered pieces.

**Why this medium:** Every t-shirt at every merch booth at every metal show is screen-printed on a Gildan blank. The menagerie shouldn't have to choose between "supporting the band" and "wearing something made with intention." Hand-dyed or hand-printed apparel is the version of merch that respects the wearer's aesthetic standards as much as the band's.

**What to look for:** Process photos showing dyeing or printing by hand. Unique color work (not replicable at scale). Small batch or one-of-a-kind pieces. Comfort with dark palettes — the HZ color world is deep violets, magentas, crimsons, golds on near-black.

**Example pitch angle:** "Every shirt at every merch booth looks the same — different logo, same blank, same print. Your process produces something that can't be replicated at scale. That's the point."

### Category 4: Visual Artist (Limited Prints)

**What:** Limited-edition prints, hand-painted variants of album art concepts (not reproductions), original pieces interpreting HZ's visual world.

**Why this medium:** Lordigan painted the Benediction artwork. The visual world of HZ is painterly, not graphic-designed. Collaborating with visual artists who work in traditional or mixed media extends the artwork's language into objects the menagerie can own. Each print is a window in the cathedral.

**What to look for:** Painterly style (not vector illustration). Dark, atmospheric, or occult-adjacent subject matter. Print editions (experience with numbered runs). Portfolio showing range within a consistent aesthetic.

**Example pitch angle:** "Lordigan painted the world our music lives in. We're looking for artists who paint adjacent worlds — different light, different architecture, same atmosphere."

### Category 5: Ceramics / Ritual Objects

**What:** Chalices, incense holders, candle vessels, altar pieces. Objects that connect to the Congregation aesthetic — the liturgical world made physical.

**Why this medium:** The entire website speaks in liturgical register. "You have been counted." "All rites reserved." "Receive the blessing." The language creates a world. Ritual objects make that world physical. A chalice from the Zoo's ceramicist is the 3D version of the website's voice — something you hold that carries the same register.

**What to look for:** Handmade ceramic work with dark, textured finishes. Ritual or ceremonial objects in their existing portfolio. Glaze work that aligns with the HZ palette (deep earth tones, metallics, no pastels). Small studio operation.

**Example pitch angle:** "Our website speaks in liturgical register — blessings, rites, offerings, the menagerie. Your ceramics already live in that world. We want to make the language physical."

## Section 8: Implementation Notes for Rubedo

*Revised 2026-04-28 per Council ace0e247. Previous version assumed HZ-side e-commerce (Stripe, inventory, order management). New model: showcase with outbound links to partner shops.*

### /relics Page Architecture

**Route:** `/relics`
**Nav:** Already defined in copy.ts as `relics: "Relics"`

**Page structure:**
1. Page header (RELICS + program description)
2. Partner grid — one card per partner, featuring the maker
3. Each partner card links outbound to the partner's own shop

**The partner is the organizing unit.** The page is a curated gallery of makers, not a storefront. Each card features the artisan — their craft, their process, their story — with a prominent outbound link: "Purchase from [Partner Name] →"

**Card treatment:** Same pattern as offerings/bestiary (CZ's consistency directive from Phase 3):
- Surface: `--hz-violet-deep` background with texture
- Border: 1px `--hz-violet`, with `--hz-gold` accent on featured/new partners
- Sharp corners (2-4px max border-radius)
- Maker's name in display type, medium in functional sans-serif
- Process description in body serif

**Images:**
- Product photography (Ray provides or maker provides)
- Maker portrait or studio shot (if available and consented)
- No rounded corners on images (stained-glass panel metaphor — sharp geometric edges)

**No e-commerce on our side.** No Stripe. No inventory tracking. No order management. No cart. No checkout. Each partner card has an outbound link to the partner's own shop where they handle everything commercial. This is the maintenance-minimization design — our site is a showcase, not a marketplace.

**For initial launch:** The /relics page launches with the empty state copy (Section 7) or with partner cards once confirmed. When a partner is confirmed, their card goes live with: name, medium, bio, process description, product photos, and a link to their shop.

```
    portfolio_url TEXT,
    pitch TEXT NOT NULL,
    email TEXT NOT NULL,
    status TEXT DEFAULT 'pending',  -- pending, reviewed, accepted, declined
    reviewed_at TEXT,
    created_at TEXT NOT NULL
);
```

**Aggregation:** Partner data aggregates to `public/data/relics.json` for the static read path (same pattern as offerings). Applications are admin-only — not public, same pattern as sanctuary submissions.

**No admin panel commerce features.** The only admin interaction with relics is managing partner cards (add/edit/deactivate) and reviewing applications. No orders, no inventory, no fulfillment tracking — those live in the partner's own systems.
